Web development is a discipline that comes with a certain set of responsibilities. Developers must provide users with information that is easily-accessible and functional. For my first journal, I decided upon the website Worldwide Elvis after passing on several (perhaps) more professional websites. The developers behind Worldwide Elvis set out to provide a service and, in doing so, clearly encountered many of the usability concerns that Steve Krug outlined in his book Don't Make Me Think.

}

'Worldwide Elvis' Purpose

As discussed in class, one of the first questions users will ask upon visiting a website is: "What is this website's purpose?". In the case of Worldwide Elvis, several elements indicate that this is a webstore. A link to 'Order Info' is clearly present at the top of the page and the 'eBay' logo (which many Internet users would be familiar with) is front and center. Perhaps less evident (discussed in more detail below, see 'Critique'), buttons on the left-hand-side redirect to pages featuring Elvis Presley merchandise and 'eBay auctions'.

The website isn't one hundred percent clear, however. The use of the acronym 'WWE' in 'WWE Policy' at the top (see screenshot above) may confuse users. While it is clearly meant to stand for Worldwide Elvis, many people may associate 'WWE' with World Wrestling Entertainment or some other popular brand.

Critique

At first glance, Worldwide Elvis appears to break Krug's first rule: "don't make me think". I believe this is largely the fault of the layout and could easily be fixed (see 'Potential Improvements'). The links at the top of the page are very informative and serve their purpose. The links on the left-hand-side, however, blend into the background and are not noticeably clickable.

The site is very easy to use - all links do what one would expect them to do. Each page loads in a timely manner and would cause a minimum amount of frustration to a reasonably Internet-savvy user (as one would expect a webstore visitor to be). The merchandise pages (i.e. where one would browse the available products) are clear and consistent.

Potential Improvements

Worldwide Elvis is on to a great start; however, all websites could be better. The first change I would make would be to the color scheme. The bright red background, while memorable, may be jarring to some users. Next, I would take Steve Krug's advice: "omit words". As mentioned above, the WWE in the 'WWE Policy' link may be doing more harm than good and it is largely unnecessary. Many of the links on the left could also benefit from this piece of advice. 'FTD' and 'BMG' are acronyms that may not be immediately apparent to all users. Categorizing merchandise under 'CDs', 'LPs', and 'EPs' could help streamline the layout.

This also goes along with Krug's idea of "satisficing" (choosing the first reasonable option). A user looking to purchase a specific CD may have to click through several categories of CD. Listing them all on one page would prevent this. Even better, the search functionality could be used more heavily. Worldwide Elvis currently supports search, but many of the products appear to not be indexed. Fleshing out the search feature and emphasizing the search bar on the front page would alleviate many of the frustrations involved in online shopping.
