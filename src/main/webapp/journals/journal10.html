For Journal 10 I chose the AAATravelView page on the U.S. Numbered Highway system. This page is one of many on the AAATravelViews website, so for my journal I'll be focusing on this page but also discussing the site as a whole.

}

'AAATravelView's Purpose

Because this page is simply one of many on the AAATravelViews website, I approached this section of my analysis from Steve Krug's trunk test scenario:

  1. (What site is this?) I was immediately aware of the "AAATravelViews" site ID in the top left-hand corner. Directly below the site ID is a tagline - "Sharing insider persepctives on travel" - which gives me a good idea of what this site is about. I was also able to look at the URL and see that this page is entitled "birth-of-the-u-s-numbered-highway-system" which is very descriptive.

  2. (Where am I?) The AAATravelViews website features bread crumbs on each page, and they are even prepended with the phrase "You are here:". At a glance I could see that: "You are here: Home > AAA Today > Birth of the U.S. Numbered Highway System". That gives me a good idea of where I am and how I could trace my way back to the home page.

  3. (What sections are available?) The AAATravelViews website maintains a succinct but descriptive set of primary navigation links that give the user a good idea of what content is available. Additionally, each primary navigation link drops down to a number of more specific links.

  4. (How can I search?) The AAATravelViews' persistent navigation contains a search bar in the upper right-hand corner of each page.

By completing Krug's trunk test, I was fairly confident that this site is successful in expressing its purpose at a glance. To be sure, I visited the home page and a few other pages and found no reason to think otherwise.

Critique

The AAATravelViews website has one of the most successful systems of persistent navigations that I have ever seen. The site ID is minimalistic (simply the name of the site in a basic font) but, combined with the tagline, is very effective. The primary navigation is responsive (that is, it changes depending on the size of the browser window) and easily accessible on any device. Notice in the following two screenshots how the design changes depending on browser size.

The site relies on a significant amount of iconography, which can be confusing for less-experienced web users. Unlike other websites that rely on similar iconography though, AAATravelView provides an "on-hover" element for each icon that displays a short description for each icon. That way, the page saves space by utilizing icons but doesn't sacrifice usability.

My major issue with the site, however, is the wordiness. These articles are meant to be informative; however, they are over 1000 words each and have only one or two images to break the monotony. I feel like the designers should either limit the number of words or introduce more graphics.

Potential Improvements

As mentioned above, I believe the site should conform to Krug's rule of "omit words". The articles are informative, but they could be shorter. Alternatively, the designers could introduce more graphics to break the monotony or split the content into more sections.
