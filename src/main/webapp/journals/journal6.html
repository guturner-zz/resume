journal_six(war_on_drugs) {

With chapter 8 and 9 of Steve Krug's book Don't Make Me Think covering usability testing, I have decided to base this week's journal on an overview of the topics discussed by Krug thus far. This includes such topics as: self-evidence, visual hierarchies, buttons, wordiness, navigation, and the home page. The Drug Policy website I chose abides by many of the conventions Krug highlights.

}

'Drug Policy's Purpose

The Drug Policy website has a very minimalistic, but effective, design. It relies heavily upon a clear visual heirarchy to guide the user's eyes. The first thing I noticed upon loading the home page was the first "screen" of the rotating welcome blurb, which succinctly states the site's purpose and presents dramatic iconography:

As the aforementioned welcome blurb states: "The Drug Enforcement Administration is fighting to block marijuana legalization and maintain the drug war status quo. That's why the Drug Policy Alliance is leading a campaign to hold the DEA accountable." Even if users happen to miss this welcome blurb before the screen changes, the menu items at the top indicate that this site hopes to educate the user about the issues related to drug policy. A red header to the right of the main content reads "Take Action" and explains in more detail that this site is anti-DEA.

Critique

The Drug Policy website features a very effective system of primary navigation. The site ID is at the top left-hand-side of each page and serves as a link to the home page. The navigation links are all simple, one word headings. The homepage even features a search bar at the top right-hand-side.

Not only does the Drug Policy website have a good system of primary navigation, it has gone the extra mile and implemented secondary navigation as an "on-hover" element below the primary navigation.

Each page on the Drug Policy website contains a title in big, red letters that perfectly mirrors the links on the secondary navigation. Not only that, but the primary navigation changes based on the page you are on (the large link is underlined in red). This makes this site a great candidate for the Krug Trunk Test.

Each of the pages on the site are short (no more than 200 words, and typically only about 100). The developers of this site clearly embrace Krug's rule of "Omit Words".

Potential Improvements

The Drug Policy website follows Krug's advice almost perfectly, there is very little I would change. The being said, I would definitely work to differentiate the links on the left-hand-side of the home page from the content in the center. This could probably be done with one of the thin grey lines used elsewhere on the homepage.

I would also turn each of the main headers on the primary navigation into a link that redirects to a summary page for each section. Even after being on the page for 15+ minutes, I keep finding myself clicking on the primary navigation elements because convention tells me that they should be links.
