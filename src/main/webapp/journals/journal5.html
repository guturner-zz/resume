For Journal 5, I chose to focus on Chapter 7 of Steve Krug's book Don't Make Me Think. The chapter, entitled "Designing the Homepage", provides a detailed outline on how to design an effective homepage. The Fox Business website maintains a relatively effective homepage that serves as a good example.

}

'Fox Business' Purpose

Upon loading the Fox Business website, my eyes were led to the flashy "Fox Business" Site ID in the top left (where one would expect it). Immediately after, I was drawn to the primary navigation options. The options "Investing", "Personal Finance", and "Business Leaders", alongside the site's title "Fox Business" help point out that this is a page for business news.

If there is any further doubt, it is cleared up by the content immediately below the primary navigation. Fox Business is set up very much like a newspaper, and the "article headings" deal with business-related news.

Critique

Fox Business's home page features a tab-style primary navigation which Krug strongly recommended in his previous chapter (Chapter 6). This is a very user-friendly and effective form of navigation. Fox Business also contains all of the elements of an effective persistent navigation system: a Site ID, primary navigation, utilities, and a search bar. This essentially guarantees that the Fox Business website will pass Krug's "trunk test".

Unfortunately, Fox Business breaks Krug's tagline rule. The tagline "The Power to Prosper" reads more like a motto and tells us nothing about the site. While the site name is very self-explanatory, some users might be thrown off by this confusing "tagline".

As mentioned above, Fox Business's visual hierarchy mimics that of a newspaper and is very effective for what the site is hoping to accomplish. Some article headings are larger than others, which clearly symbolizes that these articles are more important.

That being said, I believe Fox Business could benefit from Krug's law "Omit Words". Each of the pages is fairly overwhelming with the amount of text. Each article heading is followed by a paragraph introducing that article. This could easily be cut down to a sentence per article, which would also make the article headings more prominent.

Potential Improvements

My main improvement would be to add a secondary navigation, probably as an "on-hover" element on top of the primary navigation. As it stands right now, when a user clicks on (for example) the "Technology" heading, they are taken to a very busy page. This technology page could easily be separated into several smaller, easier-to-scan pages.

Next, I would replace the current tagline with one that actually expresses what the site is about. For example, Fox Business could use the tagline "Your First Source of Business News".
