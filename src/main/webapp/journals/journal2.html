For Journal 2, I searched for a site that was (overall) successful in adhering to Steve Krug's fundamental rules on usability. I chose the National Park Service of Detroit website because it appeared to have been created by a smaller (possibly less experienced) web developer than many of the other sites I encountered. Unlike the website I chose for Journal 1, this website is not setting out to provide a service but mainly exists for information-sharing purposes.

}

'National Park Services of Detroit's Purpose

The National Park Service of Detroit clearly establishes its purposes on the front page. An image at the top of the page attracts the user's eye and proclaims in unambiguous language: "A National Register of Historic Places".

If this isn't enough, a bolded sentence at the beginning of the second paragraph stands out among the text. It provides a description of the website, including such information as: "[This site] uses residential, recreational, commercial, industrial, and religious locations to create a tour...that documents how past and present come together".

Finally, menu items on the right-hand-side of the home page link to such pages as "Detroit Map" and "More about Detroit", clearing up any lingering ambiguity.

Critique

For the most part, the National Park Service of Detroit website adheres to Krug's first law: "Don't Make Me Think". As a user who knows nothing about Detroit, I was able to quickly browse through the provided links and access a large amount of information.

That being said, one major flaw exists in this website's design that did cause me to stumble. Each page has a non-interactive header image containing the name of the site in blue, underlined text (see screenshot below). Although this is a popular web convention for a clickable link, this text is not a link.

The website is built well for scanning. The "Learn More About Detroit" page, specifically, utilizes descriptive links as headers which allow users to spend a couple of seconds on each potential link before either making a decision or moving on to the next.

Potential Improvements

While the home page is very effective, I believe that some of the text is unnecessary. This page could benefit from some bullet points describing the intentions of the site instead of lengthy paragraphs.

The biggest issue I see with this site, however, is on the "Historic Places in Detroit" page. The page simply presents users with a list of links. While the names are fairly self explanatory, I feel that they would not be obvious to an individual who has not visited Detroit. Each link could carry with it a brief description or image.
